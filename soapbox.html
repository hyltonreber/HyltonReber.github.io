Overview

The Challenge

The Red Bull Soapbox Race is an international amateur engineering competition where teams design and build entirely gravity-powered vehicles, then race them in front of a live crowd on a purpose-built course. No motor, no stored energy — just engineering, craftsmanship, and gravity.

Axes & Axles LLC entered the 2026 Denver event with a bare chassis and six weeks to turn it into a race-ready vehicle. That meant designing and fabricating the full system from scratch: suspension geometry, steering linkage, braking, and any structural reinforcement the chassis needed. Every component had to pass a Red Bull technical inspection before we were allowed on the course.

I co-founded the LLC specifically for this event and served as team lead and lead engineer throughout the build — directing the overall design, making structural calls, and doing the majority of hands-on fabrication alongside the team.

Mechanical Design

Suspension Redesign

The chassis arrived with basic axle hardware already in place, but the suspension geometry — control arm layout, pickup points, spring mounting, and pivot angles — had to be designed and fabricated from scratch. The first design established the basic architecture: arm positions that kept the wheels roughly square to the chassis and allowed vertical travel without bottoming out against the frame rails.

Loaded and on the vehicle, the first design revealed problems that weren't apparent during the build. The geometry produced inconsistent wheel behavior through the travel range — binding in certain positions and allowing too much uncontrolled movement in others. A first full teardown addressed the worst of those issues. The second design held better, but geometry problems affecting tracking and predictability under load required another complete rebuild before the front end behaved as intended. Two Redesigns

Each rebuild targeted problems that only became clear under real load — binding through the travel range, inconsistent wheel position, and clearance issues that weren't visible off the vehicle. Two full teardowns in a six-week build window was expensive in time, but each one produced a measurably better result. The final configuration eliminated those problems and held together through the full course on race day.

Late Build · Part One

Steering Fix

Achieving Red Bull's required 7 inches of ground clearance meant inverting the spindles — and inverting the spindles inverted the steering. Turning the wheel right made the vehicle go left.

The fix was a steering arm extension mounted behind the spindle, which reversed the effective linkage geometry without requiring a full teardown and rebuild.

That fixed the direction problem — but introduced a new one. The arm extension reduced mechanical advantage in the linkage, making the steering significantly more sensitive. A separate weight-saving decision compounded it: the steering reduction gears were cut to shed 4 lbs, removing the mechanical advantage the system had been relying on to dampen driver inputs.

Corrected steering geometry after the inversion fix.

Race Day Effect

On race day the steering was still slightly toed out and oversensitive — the vehicle tracked inconsistently through the course and the team posted a slow time as a result. The direction problem was solved; the sensitivity wasn't.

Late Build · Part Two

Brake Emergency

The chassis arrived with hydraulic brakes already installed. The team got the system leak-free and briefly functional before the trip to Denver — bled, refilled, and confirmed working.

On race morning, bleeding and refilling the lines one final time, they discovered the calipers weren't actuating at all. The pistons weren't moving. About an hour of on-site troubleshooting couldn't save the hydraulic system, and it was ruled a lost cause.

The failed hydraulic caliper, pulled apart during race-morning troubleshooting.

The team had brought a full rolling toolkit to Denver — but not brake parts. With no replacement hardware available and the clock running, the fix was improvised entirely from what was on hand: spare brake pads were welded directly onto the jaws of a pair of channel-lock pliers. One arm of the pliers was welded to the frame. The other arm was extended using a disassembled pair of quick-grip clamps, welded in place so the driver could reach and operate it from the seat.

Race Day — June 13, 2026

Fabricated and installed race morning. Passed Red Bull's technical inspection. The vehicle completed the course.

The fact that it finished at all — after a hydraulic brake failure on race morning with no spare parts — was the engineering win.
