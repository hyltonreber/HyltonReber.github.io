Overview

Two Projects, One Toolset

The first project came sophomore year: a from-scratch modeling exercise built around a complete engineering drawing package for a steam engine. Every part had to be modeled individually in SolidWorks — translated from 2D drawings, with all tolerances and geometry respected — then mated into a working assembly and rendered. No shortcuts; if it was on the drawing, it had to be in the model.

The senior project shifted emphasis. Most of the course was structured around preparation for the Certified SolidWorks Expert (CSWE) exam — rigorous, exam-focused training that covered the full breadth of SolidWorks capabilities. The hands-on design component ran alongside that: taking a small steam engine that runs on the heat differential from a hot cup of coffee and redesigning the flywheel, the legs, and the motion mechanism the flywheel drives, then fabricating the new parts via 3D printing.

The two projects are different in scope and depth by design. The sophomore assembly was a sustained, end-to-end modeling exercise. The senior redesign was intentionally smaller — a vehicle for applying specific skills while the bulk of the term was invested in the CSWE.

Project One · Sophomore Year

Steam Engine Assembly

Received a complete engineering drawing package for a steam engine and built every part from scratch in SolidWorks. The mechanism is a belt-driven, gear-reduced, twin-piston design: a belt drives a pulley shaft; a spur gear and pinion step the speed down; the pinion drives a crank shaft; and the crank converts that rotation into the reciprocating motion of two pistons running in the engine body. An integrated air chamber in the casting smooths the output pressure pulses between strokes.

Parts modeled from the drawing package include the spur gear and pinion, the crank shaft, the guide rod and piston rod that constrain the piston travel, the yoke slider-crank linkage, twin pistons sealed with cup leather packing, the cast engine body with its integrated air chamber, the structural slide base with guide rod brackets, and all gaskets and fasteners. Each part was built to the dimensions and tolerances on the original drawings, then assembled with mates that capture the actual mechanism motion — meaning gear teeth had to mesh at the correct center distance, the slider-crank had to clear its housing through the full stroke, and the piston seals had to be specified to hold pressure.

Why It Mattered

This was the first time building a full multi-part assembly from a real engineering drawing package — not a guided tutorial or a single-part exercise, but a complete mechanism where every component had to be correct before any of it would work together. Translating 2D dimensioning and tolerancing standards into 3D geometry that actually meshes, slides, and seals correctly is a different skill than modeling parts in isolation. Gear teeth that have to mesh at the right center distance. A slider-crank that has to clear its own housing through the full stroke. Piston seals that have to be specified to hold pressure. Getting all of that right simultaneously is what the project was testing.

Project Two · Senior Year

Flywheel & Motion Redesign

The subject of the senior redesign is a small steam engine that runs on the heat differential between a hot cup of coffee and ambient air — a low-temperature Stirling-type device small enough to sit on a desktop. The engine already existed; the task was to improve it. Specifically: redesign the flywheel, the legs that support the engine body, and the motion mechanism the flywheel drives — then fabricate the new parts via 3D printing and assemble them onto the engine.

The redesign focused on improving the aesthetic and functional geometry of the flywheel, increasing the structural stability of the leg assembly, and refining the linkage that connects flywheel rotation to the piston or displacer motion. Each component was modeled in SolidWorks, evaluated against the existing mechanism's constraints, and then sent to a 3D printer for fabrication.

Context

Most of the senior course went to CSWE exam preparation — a comprehensive, structured training program covering the full range of SolidWorks professional capabilities. The redesign project was intentionally smaller, running alongside that exam prep rather than competing with it. That's not a disclaimer; it's a deliberate trade-off: the CSWE was the primary investment, and the redesign was the applied component that gave the certification work a concrete output. Both are on this page because they came from the same course and used the same skills.
